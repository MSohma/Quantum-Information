Holevo's commutation super operator and Tomita-Takesaki Theory

Modular operator and Holevo's commutation operator

Masaki Sohma

Introduction: Exercise of operator algebras

We will do an exercise given in the Holevo's book in a case of finite dimensional von Neumann algebra. Let us start with GNS construction for a von Neumann algebra $\mathfrak{N}=M_n(\mathbb{C})$, which is the ensemble of bounded linear operators on the vector space $\mathbb{C}^n$ with the inner product $(x,y)=x^Ty$. Let $\rho$ be a non-degenerated density operator and $\omega$ be the corresponding normal state given by $\omega(A)=\mbox{Tr}\rho A$. We introduce a Hilbert space $\mathfrak{H}=\mathfrak{N}$ with an inner product $$ \langle A, B \rangle =\omega(A^{\ast}B), $$ and consider a representation $$ \pi_l:\mathfrak{N}\to \mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H}), $$ where $\mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H})$ is the ensemble of bounded operators on $\mathfrak{H}$ and $\pi_l(A)$ is given by $\pi_l(A)B=AB$. Such a way to give a representation of von Neumann algebra $\mathfrak{N}$ is called GNS construction. In this document our interest is devoted to the von Neumann algebra $\mathfrak{M}=\pi_l(\mathfrak{N})$. For $\mathfrak{M}$, we prove the main theorem of Tomita-Takesaki theory and establish a relation between the modular operator and the Holevo's commutation operator, $$ \Delta=\left(1+\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)\left(1-\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)^{-1}. $$

Vector Representation

For a whle we consider a tracial state $\tau(A)=\frac{1}{n}\mbox{Tr}A$ on $\mathfrak{N}=M_N(\mathbb{C})$. Then the inner product on $\mathfrak{N}$ is denotedy by $\langle \cdot,\cdot\rangle_\tau$, and $\sqrt{n}e_je_k^T$ ($j,k=1,2,...,n$) constitue an orthonormal basis, where $e_j=(\delta_{j1},\delta_{j,2},...,\delta_{j,n})^T$ with the Kronecker delta $\delta_{j,l}$. We identify $\mathfrak{N}$ with $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$ using a unitary map given by $v(e_je_k^T)=e_k\otimes e_j$. Here we assume that $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$ has an inner product $$ (f_1\otimes g_1, f_2\otimes g_2 ) =\frac{1}{n}(f_1,g_1)(f_2,g_2) $$ for which the orthonormal basis is given by $\{\sqrt{n}e_k\otimes e_j;k,j=1,2,...n\}$.

For $A\in \mathfrak{N}$, $\pi_l(A)$ is represented as $\tilde{\pi}_l(A)=I_n\otimes A$ on $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$. In fact $$ \begin{split} v(\pi_l(A)e_je_k^T)&=v(Ae_je_k^T)=e_k\otimes A e_j\\ &= (I_n\otimes A)e_k\otimes e_j=\tilde{\pi}_l(A)v(e_je_k^T), \end{split} $$ and hence $\pi_l(A)=v^\ast\tilde{\pi}_l(A)v$. Note that, in the field of matrix calculus, we identify $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$ with $\mathbb{C}^{n^2}$ by using the basis $\{e_k\otimes e_j;k,j=1,2,...n\}$, and call the operator $v$ a vec operator or a col operator. On the other hand, $\pi_r(A):\mathfrak{N}\in X \to XA\ni\mathfrak{N}$ is represented as $\tilde{\pi}_r(A)=A^T\otimes I_n$ on $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$. In fact, $$ \begin{split} v(\pi_r(A)e_je_k^T)&=v(e_je_k^TA)=A^Te_k\otimes e_j\\ &= (A^T\otimes I_n)e_k\otimes e_j=\tilde{\pi}_r(A)v(e_je_k^T), \end{split} $$ and hence $\pi_r(A)=v^\ast\tilde{\pi}_r(A) v$.

In order to deal with anti-linear operators on $\mathfrak{H}=\mathfrak{N}$, we view $\mathfrak{H}$ as a real Hilbert space. A complex matrix $A=(a_{j,k})\in \mathfrak{N}=M_n(\mathbb{C})$ with $$a_{j,k}=x_{j,k}+iy_{j,k}$$ corresponds to a real matrix $\tilde{A}=(\tilde{a}_{j,k})\in M_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$ with $$ \tilde{a}_{j,k}= \begin{pmatrix} x_{j,k}&-y_{j,k}\\ y_{j,k}&x_{j,k} \end{pmatrix}\in M_2(\mathbb{R}). $$ Here we can take a natural basis $\{ \sqrt{n}e_je_k^T\otimes I_2,\sqrt{n}e_je_k^T\otimes J_2\}$ with the $2\times 2$-identity matrix $I_2$ and $$ J_2=\begin{pmatrix} 0&-1\\ 1&0 \end{pmatrix}. $$ The ensemble of such real matrices is denoted by $M^r_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$; $M^r_{2n}(\mathbb{R})=\{\tilde{A}\in M_{2n}(\mathbb{R}) ; A\in M_n(\mathbb{C})\}$. In addition, we define a bijection $$v_r:M^r_{2n}(\mathbb{R})\to \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^2$$ by $$ \begin{split} v_r(e_je_k^T\otimes I_2)&=e_k\otimes e_j\otimes f_1,\\ v_r(e_je_k^T\otimes J_2)&=e_k\otimes e_j\otimes f_2, \end{split} $$ with $f_1=(1,0)^T,f_2=(0,1)^T$. Thus we have obtained convenient relations $$ M_n(\mathbb{C})\simeq \mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n\simeq M_{2n}^r(\mathbb{R})\simeq \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^2. $$

Expression on $\mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes\mathbb{R}^2$

The transpose operation $\mathfrak{N}\in A \to A^T\ni \mathfrak{N}$ can be represented on $\mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes\mathbb{R}^2 $ by $O_T\otimes I_2$, where $O_T$ is a operator on $\mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n$ given by $$O_T(e_k\otimes e_j)= e_j\otimes e_k.$$ The $\ast$-operation on $\mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^2$ is given by $O_{\ast}=O_T\otimes K_2$ with $$ K_2=\begin{pmatrix} 1&0\\ 0&-1 \end{pmatrix}. $$ The operators $\pi_l(A)$ and $\pi_r(A)$ introduced in the previous section are represented on $\mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^n\otimes \mathbb{R}^2$ as $\tilde{\pi}_l(A)\otimes I_2$ and $\tilde{\pi}_r(A)\otimes I_2$ respectively.

Inner product on a $\ast$-algebra $\mathfrak{A}$

$A,B\in\mathfrak{A}$

  1. $\langle A,B \rangle_{-} = \omega(A^{\ast}B)$ , which is used in the GNS representation
  2. $\langle A,B \rangle_{+} = \omega(BA^{\ast})$
  3. $\langle A,B \rangle = \omega(\frac{1}{2}(BA^{\ast}+A^{\ast}B))$

We can consider $\mathfrak{A}$ as a Hilbert space in three different ways. Hilbert spaces with these inner products are denoted by $H_{-},H_{+},H$ respectively.

Properties of $\langle \cdot,\cdot \rangle$

$A,B\in\mathfrak{A}$

  1. Involution $A\to A^*$ is an anti-unitary operator, i.e. $\langle A^{\ast},B^{\ast}\rangle=\overline{\langle A,B\rangle}$
  2. Inner product $\langle A,B \rangle$ give a real inner product on the adjoint set $\mathfrak{A}_S$ of $\mathfrak{A}$, where $\mathfrak{A}_S:=\{A\in\mathfrak{A};A=A^\ast\}$

With the inner product $\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle$, $\mathfrak{A}_S$ can be considered as a real Hilbert space, which is denoted by $H_S$.

Matrix expression for GNS representation

Let us consider a vec operator $$ \mbox{vec}:H_{-}\ni X= (x_1,...,x_n) \to \mbox{vec}(X)= \begin{pmatrix} x_{1}\\ x_{2}\\ :\\ x_{n} \end{pmatrix} $$ To get a matrix expression for GNS representation let us consider a map, $$ \mbox{vec}(X)\to\mbox{vec}(\pi_l(A)X), $$ where we have $$ \mbox{vec}(\pi_l(A)X)=\mbox{vec}(AX)=(I_n\otimes A)\mbox{vec}(X), $$ with a $n\times n$ identity matrix $I_n$.

This means that a matrix expression of $\pi_l(A)$ is given by $I_n\otimes A$:

$$ \pi_l(A)\sim I_n\otimes A \in M_{n^2}(\mathbb{C}). $$

Uncertainty Relation

we consider the following bilinear form on $\mathfrak{A}$.

$$ [A,B]=i\omega(A^{\ast}B-BA^{\ast}),\quad A,B\in\mathfrak{A} $$

Then it holds that

$$ \begin{split} \langle A,A\rangle\geq\pm\frac{i}{2}[A,A],A\in H\\ \langle A,A \rangle +\langle B,B \rangle\geq [A,B],A,B\in H_S\\ \langle A,A \rangle \langle B,B \rangle\geq \frac{1}{4}[A,B]^2,A,B\in H_S \end{split} $$

The last inequality gives the most general form of uncertainty relation.

Holevo's commutation operator

The anti-Hermitian operator satifying the following conditions is called commutation operator.

$$ [A,X]=\langle A, \mathfrak{D}X\rangle, \quad A,X\in H $$

Let us obtain a matrix expression of commutation operator.

Since $$ \begin{split} [A,X]&=i\omega(A^\ast X-XA^\ast)=i\mbox{Tr}\rho(A^\ast X-XA^\ast)\\ &=\mbox{Tr}A^\ast i(X\rho-\rho X)\\ \langle A,Y\rangle&=\omega((YA^\ast+A^\ast Y)/2)=\mbox{Tr}\rho((YA^\ast+A^\ast Y)/2)\\ &=\mbox{Tr}A^\ast(\rho Y+Y\rho)/2, \end{split} $$

we have $$ (\rho Y+Y\rho)/2=i(X\rho-\rho X), $$ which can be written by a vec operator as

$$ (I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)\mbox{vec}(Y)=2i(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho )\mbox{vec}(X). $$ Thus $$ \mbox{vec}(Y)=2i(I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho )\mbox{vec}(X), $$ which means that the matrix expression of $\mathfrak{D}$ is given by $$ \mathfrak{D}\sim 2i(I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho ) $$
Moreover $$ \begin{split} 1+\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}&\sim 2(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-1} I_2\otimes \rho\\ 1-\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}&\sim 2(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}\rho \otimes I_2\\ 1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}^2&\sim 4(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-2}\rho \otimes \rho\\ \end{split} $$

Properties of commutation operator

  • $1\pm\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\geq 0$
  • $1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}\geq 0$
  • $\omega$is fatihful$\Leftrightarrow$ $1\pm\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}$, $1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}^2$ is non-degenerated.

Modular operator

We explain results of Tomita-Takesaki theory in the case of $\mathfrak{M}=\pi_l(\mathfrak{N})$ with $\mathfrak{N}=M_n(\mathbb{C})$, where all difficulties in the theory vanish. We also assume that $\pi_l:\mathfrak{N}\to\mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H}_\tau)$ is the GNS representation for the tracial state $\tau(A)=\frac{1}{n}\mbox{Tr} A$. Here $\mathfrak{H}_\tau=\mathfrak{N}\simeq \mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$, $\mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H}_\tau)$ is the ensemble of linear maps on $\mathbb{C}^n\otimes \mathbb{C}^n$ and $\mathfrak{M}=\pi_l(\mathfrak{N})\simeq I_n\otimes\mathfrak{N}$. Then we have $$ \mathfrak{M}^\prime\simeq \{I_n\}^\prime\otimes\mathfrak{N}^\prime=\mathfrak{N}\otimes \{I_n\} $$ and hence $\mathfrak{M}^\prime=\pi_r(\mathfrak{N})$. In the following we use $\rho^{1/2}$ as a cyclic and separating vector $\Omega\in \mathfrak{H}_\tau$; $$ \mathfrak{H}_\tau=\mathfrak{M}\Omega=\mathfrak{M}^\prime \Omega, \quad \Omega=\rho^{1/2}. $$

Consider anti-linear operators on $\mathfrak{H}_{\tau}$ $$ \begin{split} S&:\mathfrak{M}\Omega\ni \pi_l(A) \Omega \to \pi_l(A)^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{M}\Omega\\ F&:\mathfrak{M}^\prime \Omega\ni \pi_r(A) \Omega \to \pi_r(A)^\ast\Omega\in \mathfrak{M}^\prime \Omega. \end{split} $$ The linear operator $\Delta=FS$ on $\mathfrak{H}_{\tau}$ is called a modular operator.

For the operator $S$, we have $$ S(X)=\rho^{-1/2}X^*\rho^{1/2}. $$ In fact, since $S(A\rho^{1/2})=A^\ast \rho^{1/2},\quad A\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$, we get $$ Y=(X\rho^{-1/2})^\ast \rho^{1/2}=\rho^{-1/2}X^\ast\rho^{1/2}, $$ by putting $X=A\rho^{1/2}$,$Y=A^\ast \rho^{1/2}$.

Anti-linear map $S$

$S:\mathfrak{N}\Omega\ni N \Omega \to N^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{N}\Omega,\quad \mathfrak{N}=\pi_l(\mathfrak{A})$

$\pi_l(A)\Omega=A\rho^{1/2},\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_2(\mathbb{C})$

Putting $X=A\rho^{1/2}$,$Y=A^\ast \rho^{1/2}$, we get that is,

$\pi_l(\mathfrak{A})^\prime$

Since $ \pi_l(A)\sim I_n \otimes A \in M_{n^2}(\mathbb{C}),\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_n(\mathbb{C}), $ we get $$ \pi_l(\mathfrak{A})\sim I_n \otimes \mathfrak{A}. $$ Therefore $$ \pi_l(\mathfrak{A})^{\prime}\sim \mathfrak{A}\otimes I_n. $$

On the other hand, when we define the representation $$ \tilde{\pi_l}:\mathfrak{A}\to \mathfrak{B}(H_{-}) $$ by $\tilde{\pi_l}(A)B=BA^T$, its matrix expression is given as $A\otimes I_n$.

Anti-linear operator $F$

$F:\mathfrak{N}^\prime\Omega\ni M \Omega \to M^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{N}^{\prime}\Omega,\quad \mathfrak{N}^{\prime}=\tilde{\pi_l}(\mathfrak{A})$

$\tilde{\pi_l}(A)\Omega=\rho^{1/2}A^T,\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_2(\mathbb{C})$

$F(\rho^{1/2}A^T)= \rho^{1/2}(A^T)^\ast,\quad A\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$

Putting $X=\rho^{1/2}A^T$,$Y= \rho^{1/2}(A^T)^\ast$ we get $$ Y=\rho^{1/2}(\rho^{-1/2}X)^\ast =\rho^{1/2}X^\ast\rho^{-1/2}, $$ that is, $$ F(X)=\rho^{1/2}X^*\rho^{-1/2} $$

Matrix Expression of modular operator

A complex matrix $A=(a_{j,k})\in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ corresponds to a real matrix $\tilde{A}=(\tilde{a}_{j,k})\in M_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$ with $$ \tilde{a}_{j,k}= \begin{pmatrix} x_{j,k}&-y_{j,k}\\ y_{j,k}&x_{j,k} \end{pmatrix}\in M_2(\mathbb{R}). $$ Then we can take a natural basis $\{ e_ie_j^T\otimes I_2,e_ie_j^T\otimes J_2\}$ with a $2\times 2$-identity matrix $I_2$ and $$ J_2=\begin{pmatrix} 0&-1\\ 1&0 \end{pmatrix}. $$ The ensemble of such real matrices is denoted by $M^r_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$.

A complex matrix $A=(a_{j,k})\in M_n(\mathbb{C})$ corresponds to a real matrix $\tilde{A}=(\tilde{a}_{j,k})\in M_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$ with $$ \tilde{a}_{j,k}= \begin{pmatrix} x_{j,k}&-y_{j,k}\\ y_{j,k}&x_{j,k} \end{pmatrix}\in M_2(\mathbb{R}). $$ Then we can take a natural basis $\{ e_ie_j^T\otimes I_2,e_ie_j^T\otimes J_2\}$ with a $2\times 2$-identity matrix $I_2$ and $$ J_2=\begin{pmatrix} 0&-1\\ 1&0 \end{pmatrix}. $$ The ensemble of such real matrices is denoted by $M^r_{2n}(\mathbb{R})$.

Since $ S(X)=\rho^{-1/2}X^*\rho^{1/2}, \quad F(X)=\rho^{1/2}X^*\rho^{-1/2}, $

$ \Delta(X)=F(S(X))=\rho X \rho^{-1}. $

Thus the matrix expression of modular operator is give as $$ \Delta \sim \rho^{-1}\otimes \rho $$

Relation between modular operator and commutation operator

We get $$ \Delta=\left(1+\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)\left(1-\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)^{-1} $$

Similarly $$ \frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}=(1-\Delta)(1+\Delta)^{-1} $$

Main theorem in Tomita-Takesaki Theory

We have $$ \Delta^{it}\mathfrak{M}\Delta^{-it}=\mathfrak{M},\quad \Delta^{it}\mathfrak{M}^{\prime}\Delta^{-it}=\mathfrak{M}^{\prime} $$ $$ J\mathfrak{M}J=\mathfrak{M}^{\prime} $$
