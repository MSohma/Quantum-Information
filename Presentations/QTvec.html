$\ast$ algebra

We call a complex vector space $\mathfrak{A}$ satisfying the following properties $\ast$ algebra.
  • For any $A,B\in \mathfrak{A}$ we can define the product $AB\in\mathfrak{A}$:
    1. $A(BC)=(AB)C$
    2. $A(B+C)=AB+AC,(A+B)C=AC+BC$
    3. $\alpha(AB)=A(\alpha B)=(\alpha A)B,\alpha\in \mathbb{C}$
  • For any $A\in\mathfrak{A}$ we can define the involution $A^\ast\in\mathfrak{A}$:
    1. $(A^\ast)^\ast=A,(AB)^\ast=B^\ast A^\ast, B\in\mathfrak{A}$
    2. $(\alpha A+\beta B)^\ast=\bar{\alpha} A^\ast +\bar{\beta} B^\ast$,$\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{C}$

Examples of $\ast$ algebra

  • $M_n(\mathbb{C})$:full matrix algebra of all $n\times n$ complex matrices.
  • $\mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H})$:algebra of bounded operators on a Hilbert space $\mathfrak{H}$
  • von Neumann algebra $\mathfrak{M}$: $\ast$ algebra $\mathfrak{M}\subset \mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H})$ satisfying $\mathfrak{M}^{\prime\prime}=\mathfrak{M}$, where $\mathfrak{M}^{\prime}=\{A\in \mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H})|AB=BA,\forall A\in\mathfrak{M}\}$ $\mathfrak{M}^{\prime\prime}=\{A\in \mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{H})|AB=BA,\forall A\in\mathfrak{M}^{\prime}\}$
We can also consider $C^\ast$ algebra, which is a broader concept than von Neumann algebra.

Self-adjoint element

$\mathfrak{A}$:$\ast$ algebra
  • We call $A\in\mathfrak{A}$ satisfying $A=A^\ast$ a self-adjoint element.
  • The self-adoint elements represent observables in the quantum physics.
  • $\mathfrak{A}_S:=\{A\in\mathfrak{A};A=A^\ast \}$
    • 　
  • $\mathfrak{A}\ni A=A_{{re}}+iA_{{im}}$ $$ \begin{split} A_{{re}}:=&\frac{A+A^\ast}{2}\in\mathfrak{A}_S：\mbox{real part of }A\\ A_{{im}}:=&\frac{A-A^\ast}{2i}\in\mathfrak{A}_S：\mbox{imaginary part of }A \end{split} $$

State

$\mathfrak{A}$:$\ast$ algebra with the identity $I$

A linear functional $\omega:\mathfrak{A}\to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying the following conditions is called a state.

  • $\omega(A^\ast A)\geq 0,\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}$
  • $\omega(I)=1$

When $\omega(A^{\ast}A)=0\Leftrightarrow A=0$, $\omega$ is called faithful.

It holds that $\omega(A^\ast)=\overline{\omega(A)}$.

Normal State

$\mathfrak{A}$ : von Neumann algebra

$\rho$ : density operator ($\rho\geq 0,\mbox{Tr}\rho=1$)

The state defined as

$$ \omega(A)=\mbox{Tr}\rho A $$ is called a normal state.

Representation

$\mathfrak{A},\mathfrak{B}$ : $\ast$ algebra

  • A linear map $\phi:\mathfrak{A}\to\mathfrak{B}$ satisfying the following conditions is called a $\ast$homomorphism.
    1. $\phi(AB)=\phi(A)\phi(B),\quad A,B\in \mathfrak{A}$
    2. $\phi(A^\ast)=\phi(A)^\ast$
  • A $\ast$ homomorphism $\pi:\mathfrak{A}\to \mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{L})$ is called representation on $\mathfrak{A}$,where $\mathfrak{B}(\mathfrak{L})$ is a set of bounded operators on $\mathfrak{L}$.

Remark

We consider only the following cases.

    　
  • $\mathfrak{M}=M_n(\mathbb{C})$
    1. $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ is a von Neumann algebra on a $n$-dimensional Hilbert space.
    2. It is easy to extend to the case of a finite dimenstional von Neumann albebra since it is $\ast$ isomorphic to $M_{m_1}(\mathbb{C})\otimes \cdots \otimes M_{m_n}(\mathbb{C})$.
  • We suppose that $\omega$ is a faithful normal state on $M_n(\mathbb{C})$
    1. $\omega(A)=\mbox{Tr} \rho A$, where $\rho$ is a density operator with eigenvalue $s_j>0,j=1,..,n$.
    2. It is easy to extend to the case of a finite dimensional von Neumann algebra $\mathfrak{M}$.

Inner product on a $\ast$ algebra $\mathfrak{A}$

$A,B\in\mathfrak{A}$

  1. $\langle A,B \rangle_{-} = \omega(A^{\ast}B)$ , which is used in the GNS representation
  2. $\langle A,B \rangle_{+} = \omega(BA^{\ast})$
  3. $\langle A,B \rangle = \omega(\frac{1}{2}(BA^{\ast}+A^{\ast}B))$

We can consider $\mathfrak{A}$ as a Hilbert space in three different ways. Hilbert spaces with these inner products are denoted by $H_{-},H_{+},H$ respectively.

Properties of $\langle A,B \rangle$

$A,B\in\mathfrak{A}$

  1. Involution $A\to A^*$ is a anti-unitary operator, i.e. $\langle A^{\ast},B^{\ast}\rangle=\overline{\langle A,B\rangle}$
  2. Inner product $\langle A,B \rangle$ give a real inner product on the adjoint set of $\mathfrak{A}$,$\mathfrak{A}_S$, where $\mathfrak{A}_S:=\{A\in\mathfrak{A};A=A^\ast\}$

With the inner product $\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle$,$\mathfrak{A}_S$ can be considered as a real Hilbert space, which is denoted by $H_S$.

Matrix expression for GNS representation

Let us consider a vec operator $$ \mbox{vec}:H_{-}\ni X= (x_1,...,x_n) \to \mbox{vec}(X)= \begin{pmatrix} x_{1}\\ x_{2}\\ :\\ x_{n} \end{pmatrix} $$

To get a matrix expression for GNS representation let us consider a map, $$ \mbox{vec}(X)\to\mbox{vec}(\pi(A)X), $$ where we have $$ \mbox{vec}(\pi(A)X)=\mbox{vec}(AX)=(I_n\otimes A)\mbox{vec}(X), $$ with a $n\times n$ identity matrix $I_n$.

This means that a matrix expression of $\pi(A)$ is given by $I_n\otimes A$:

$$ \pi(A)\sim I_n\otimes A \in M_{n^2}(\mathbb{C}). $$

Uncertainty Relation

we consider the following bilinear form on $\mathfrak{A}$.

$$ [A,B]=i\omega(A^{\ast}B-BA^{\ast}),\quad A,B\in\mathfrak{A} $$

Then it holds that

$$ \begin{split} \langle A,A\rangle\geq\pm\frac{i}{2}[A,A],A\in H\\ \langle A,A \rangle +\langle B,B \rangle\geq [A,B],A,B\in H_S\\ \langle A,A \rangle \langle B,B \rangle\geq \frac{1}{4}[A,B]^2,A,B\in H_S \end{split} $$

The last inequality gives the most general form of uncertainty relation.

Holevo's commutation operator

The anti-Hermitian operator satifying the following conditions is called commutation operator.

$$ [A,X]=\langle A, \mathfrak{D}X\rangle, \quad A,X\in H $$

Let us obtain a matrix expression of commutation operator.

Since $$ \begin{split} [A,X]&=i\omega(A^\ast X-XA^\ast)=i\mbox{Tr}\rho(A^\ast X-XA^\ast)\\ &=\mbox{Tr}A^\ast i(X\rho-\rho X)\\ \langle A,Y\rangle&=\omega((YA^\ast+A^\ast Y)/2)=\mbox{Tr}\rho((YA^\ast+A^\ast Y)/2)\\ &=\mbox{Tr}A^\ast(\rho Y+Y\rho)/2, \end{split} $$

we have $$ (\rho Y+Y\rho)/2=i(X\rho-\rho X), $$ which can be written by a vec operator as

$$ (I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)\mbox{vec}(Y)=2i(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho )\mbox{vec}(X). $$ Thus $$ \mbox{vec}(Y)=2i(I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho )\mbox{vec}(X), $$ which means that the matrix expression of $\mathfrak{D}$ is given by $$ \mathfrak{D}\sim 2i(I_2\otimes \rho + \rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}(\rho\otimes I_2 -I_2\otimes \rho ) $$
Moreover $$ \begin{split} 1+\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}&\sim 2(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-1} I_2\otimes \rho\\ 1-\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}&\sim 2(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-1}\rho \otimes I_2\\ 1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}^2&\sim 4(I_2\otimes \rho+\rho\otimes I_2)^{-2}\rho \otimes \rho\\ \end{split} $$

Properties of commutation operator

  • $1\pm\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\geq 0$
  • $1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}\geq 0$
  • $\omega$is fatihful$\Leftrightarrow$ $1\pm\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}$, $1+\frac{1}{4}\mathfrak{D}^2$ is non-degenerated.

Modular operator

$\mathfrak{A}=M_n(\mathbb{C})$

$\omega(A)=\mbox{Tr}\rho A$: faithful state on $\mathfrak{A}$

$\pi:\mathfrak{A}\to \mathfrak{B}(H_{-})$ : GNS representation for $\omega$

$\mathfrak{N}=\pi(\mathfrak{A})$ is a von Neumann algebra.

Let us consider $\rho^{1/2}$ as a cyclic and separating vector $\Omega$. $$H_{-}=\mathfrak{N}\Omega=\mathfrak{N}^\prime \Omega$$

$\bullet$ Consider anti-linear operoator on $H_{-}$, $S$ and $F$ $$ \begin{split} S&:\mathfrak{N}\Omega\ni N \Omega \to N^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{N}\Omega\\ F&:\mathfrak{N}^\prime \Omega\ni M\Omega \to M^\ast\Omega\in \mathfrak{N}^\prime \Omega \end{split} $$

$\bullet$ The linear operator on $H_{-}$ defined as follows is called a modular operator. $$ \Delta=FS $$

Anti-linear map $S$

$S:\mathfrak{N}\Omega\ni N \Omega \to N^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{N}\Omega,\quad \mathfrak{N}=\pi(\mathfrak{A})$

$\pi(A)\Omega=A\rho^{1/2},\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_2(\mathbb{C})$

$S(A\rho^{1/2})=A^\ast \rho^{1/2},\quad A\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$

Putting $X=A\rho^{1/2}$,$Y=A^\ast \rho^{1/2}$, we get $$ Y=(X\rho^{-1/2})^\ast \rho^{1/2}=\rho^{-1/2}X^\ast\rho^{1/2}, $$ that is, $$ S(X)=\rho^{-1/2}X^*\rho^{1/2} $$

$\pi(\mathfrak{A})^\prime$

Since $ \pi(A)\sim I_n \otimes A \in M_{n^2}(\mathbb{C}),\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_n(\mathbb{C}), $ we get $$ \pi(\mathfrak{A})\sim I_n \otimes \mathfrak{A}. $$ Therefore $$ \pi(\mathfrak{A})^{\prime}\sim \mathfrak{A}\otimes I_n. $$

On the other hand, when we define the representation $$ \tilde{\pi}:\mathfrak{A}\to \mathfrak{B}(H_{-}) $$ by $\tilde{\pi}(A)B=BA^T$, its matrix expression is given as $A\otimes I_n$.

Anti-linear operator $F$

$F:\mathfrak{N}^\prime\Omega\ni M \Omega \to M^\ast\Omega\in\mathfrak{N}^{\prime}\Omega,\quad \mathfrak{N}^{\prime}=\tilde{\pi}(\mathfrak{A})$

$\tilde{\pi}(A)\Omega=\rho^{1/2}A^T,\quad A\in \mathfrak{A}=M_2(\mathbb{C})$

$F(\rho^{1/2}A^T)= \rho^{1/2}(A^T)^\ast,\quad A\in M_2(\mathbb{C})$

Putting $X=\rho^{1/2}A^T$,$Y= \rho^{1/2}(A^T)^\ast$ we get $$ Y=\rho^{1/2}(\rho^{-1/2}X)^\ast =\rho^{1/2}X^\ast\rho^{-1/2}, $$ that is, $$ F(X)=\rho^{1/2}X^*\rho^{-1/2} $$

Matrix expression of modular operator

Since $ S(X)=\rho^{-1/2}X^*\rho^{1/2}, \quad F(X)=\rho^{1/2}X^*\rho^{-1/2}, $

$ \Delta(X)=F(S(X))=\rho X \rho^{-1}. $

Thus the matrix expression of modular operator is give as $$ \Delta \sim \rho^{-1}\otimes \rho $$

Relation between modular operator and commutation operator

We get $$ \Delta=\left(1+\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)\left(1-\frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}\right)^{-1} $$

Similarly $$ \frac{i}{2}\mathfrak{D}=(1-\Delta)(1+\Delta)^{-1} $$
